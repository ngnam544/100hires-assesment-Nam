Herzliya Pituach is one of the most underrated places in Israel for people working in tech.

Here’s why:

Most people associate Israeli tech with Tel Aviv.

What many people don't realize is that before Sarona became the center of the startup ecosystem, a huge amount of Israel's tech industry was based in Herzliya.

And a lot of that ecosystem is still there today.

Apple, Microsoft, AppsFlyer, and a number of prominent venture capital firms like Pitango, Viola Ventures, and Glilot Capital all have a significant presence in the area.

The second advantage is accessibility.

Herzliya Pituach is an incredible location if you work in Tel Aviv.

It's roughly a 15-18 minute drive without traffic and even during rush hour, it's often closer to 25-30 minutes than an hour.

And in fact, the Tel Aviv light rail is being extended to Herzliya, which will make the connection even easier.

The third advantage is that people are willing to come to you because Herzliya Pituach is such a beautiful place. As I covered in my previous post, between the beach, restaurants, and atmosphere, it's an appealing place to spend some time.

I had numerous meetings in Herzliya where people travelled in from Tel Aviv, Ra'anana, and elsewhere in central Israel.

So if you work in tech, moving to Herzliya Pituach might be a great option for you.

It's home to many of the companies and investors that have built the tech ecosystem and it's still one of the easiest places from which to access Tel Aviv.

David Siegel

Herzliya Pituach was the final community we tried out.

And it highlighted something really important. When choosing where to live, you're often balancing two different things:

Lifestyle and community.

When it comes to lifestyle, Herzliya Pituach is hard to beat.

You have the amazing beaches, great restaurants, easy access to Tel Aviv, and a more relaxed atmosphere. There’s still plenty of energy around you, but without the high intensity that comes with living in central Tel Aviv.

I can absolutely see why so many people love it here.

However, from my experience, this may not be as strong a community for 30-55 year old Anglos moving to Israel without a strong existing network.

First, it’s a lot more ‘Israeli’ than all the other communities I tried out.

There are diplomats, Israelis, Anglos, and people from all over the world. It’s less of an Anglo bubble and more like a diverse international community. For many this is ideal.

Second, my impression was that most people here already have their established circles, friendships, and routines.

No one was trying to ‘recruit’ us like countless people tried to in places like Zichron Yaakov and Tel Mond.

That’s not necessarily a criticism, and for many it is a positive. It's just a different dynamic here.

And after spending time in so many different communities, I understand even more clearly that there’s no such thing as a perfect community.

Only the community that’s the best fit for you.

In my next post, I'll share why Herzliya Pituach might actually be one of the most underrated places in Israel for people working in tech.

One of my biggest hacks for integrating into a new community is simple:

Join as many WhatsApp groups as possible.

I’m probably in over 100 groups at this point.

Every time we spend time in a new city I join:

- the pickleball and hiking WhatsApp groups

- the board games WhatsApp group

- the synagogue WhatsApp groups

- and more…..

There’s a few reasons why.

Number one, practically speaking, you find out what events are going on so you can actually meet people and integrate into the community faster.

Number two. You learn a huge amount about a community just by observing how people communicate in those groups.

For example, one synagogue group only posted about young children’s events. That told us that this was primarily for families with young kids and not a great fit for us.

Number three. You get a feel for how Israeli vs Anglo a community is. Some groups are entirely in Hebrew, some in English, and some are a mix.

And yes it can get chaotic being part of so many groups, so make sure to mute notifications and put them in archive.

I know there are also a lot of great Facebook groups out there, but in my opinion WhatsApp groups are the hidden engine of community life in Israel.

David Siegel

Former CEO of Meetup & Investopedia (7x and 5x exits). Author of Decide & Conquer, Tel Aviv University Professor, Strategy Advisor, CEO Coach. Founder & Chairman, Israel Tech Mission

One mistake people make when choosing where to live is that they want everything.

They want:

- the beauty and affordability of a small town

- the infrastructure and convenience of a big city

- a strong community

- nonstop things to do

- a short commute

- lots of space

The problem is that you can’t compare apples and oranges.

You can’t look for the benefits of a big city in a small city.

And you can’t look for the benefits of a small city in a big city.

Big cities come with certain advantages:

- more things to do

- better transportation

- more energy

- more anonymity

Smaller towns come with different advantages:

- stronger social fabric

- more space

- more affordability

- a more bucolic lifestyle

Part of moving cities or countries successfully is understanding what type of environment actually fits your personality and stage of life.

Some people thrive in busy, intense environments.

Other people would happily give that up for nature, community, and a slower pace of life.

Neither is right or wrong.

Just know that no place gives you everything.

The key is being honest with what type of life you actually want.
