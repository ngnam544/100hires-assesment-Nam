hi i'm david and i'm a helpaholic.  

that's right... i'm addicted to helping. 

you might be thinking, "my friend, what's wrong with helping?" 

well, amigo, nothing is wrong with helping. but being addicted to helping? not great. 

what does it mean to be addicted to helping?

- i attach my self worth to people seeing me as helpful 

- i try to play the hero, even if it disempowers people from helping themselves. 

- i struggle to just be still. if i'm not *doing* something that's helping, i feel anxious and like my life lacks meaning

and i know i'm not the only one. i see this happen allll the time in the world of mission-driven startups, community building, coaching, therapy, and other people-focused work. 

it's a sneaky one, because helping seems like such an objectively good thing. it's not like drugs or alcohol or gambling which society deems as bad. 

if anything we see it as righteous and use it to put ourselves on a pedestal above others. "see how much i'm helping?? that makes me better than others. they just care about power. but not me. i'm a helper!"

one thing to pay attention to: is it more important that you help, or that you're seen as someone who helps?

i know for me, just helping doesn't often feel like it's enough. i want to be seen as being helpful, even if just by the person i'm helping.

yeah... it's tough.

and it's so easy to disregard an addiction to helping. to being the hero. to having a positive impact in the world. and to miss how it's actually causing suffering for you and others.

it can still be an attachment. it can still be a way of gaining power over others. and it's still a way we prove to ourselves that we are not enough. that our enoughness has to be earned. 

if our helping isn't clean, it's not for them, it's for us. it's a way of avoiding our suffering. but that suffering doesn't go away. it festers and it bleeds out into everything we do, even the "good" things we do to "help" others. 

here's the good news...

when we see that story, shine a light on it, and hold space for the parts of us that think we have to help in order to be enough, worthy, and safe, those parts soften, and we realize that we don't have to prove anything. we're already enough, whether or not we help.

and we realize we can still help, but from a place of open-hearted surrender. from a place of love and enoughness. we can show up, stand up for our values, and do right by the people around us, without making their well-being our responsibility, without tying our self worth to the role of helping. and without making "helper" a badge of status we get to wear. 

for my fellow helpaholics out there, i see and appreciate you. we mean well. we have big hearts. i'm here figuring this out with you.

a stance i see a lot of community builders take is being the "savior" of members. it seems noble but it can lead to whole lot of suffering...

i know because it's a stance i've taken as a community builder before, and it's an edge i often work on with clients.

where this comes from is that at some point, we developed a pattern of being the savior as a way to feel worthy of love. as a way to feel like we are enough. or a way to feel empowered in moments where our autonomy was threatened.

for me, i was never a fighter, i was never "cool", and so being a "helper" or a "good kid" became my method for feeling significant in the eyes of my parents, other adults, teachers, peers, etc.

in grade school, this was quite effective. but this pattern keeps playing out long after it's no longer needed. now, as adults, we're still playing the savior role as a way to fill a hole of our own not-enoughness. but then helping becomes more about us, about healing our own wounds, rather than acting truly for others.

and we see this play out for community builders in lots of ways:

the community manager who believes the business is bad and extractive, and make it their job to defend the members from their own employer

the community founder who needs to be the one to solve all the members' problems, and can't handle situations where members are struggling or angry without trying to fix it

the community leader who has to be the center of the movement, being seen as the savior

(again, raising my hand here who has done all of these things, so no judgment).

this is a very difficult place to be. a whole lot of weight on the shoulders of the community builder. they might feel like they can't let any of the members down. that it's a personal failure when people are upset. that the well-being of each community member is up to them. that members can't defend or take care of themselves without them.

this is a fast track to burnout and disempowerment for you and your members.

instead, what would it look like to serve from a place of enoughness? to not attach your self-worth to the wellbeing of others, but rather just show up with your whole heart, with care, and with presence to the work. to live your values as best you can, and know that you are enough, regardless of the outcome.

I started TOMS at 29. Sold it at 43. Fell into the deepest depression of my life at 44. And I'm 49 now. Here are the lessons I wish I'd learned:

#1: Doing more won't make you feel enough.

At 29, I thought achievement was the answer. If I just helped more people, gave away more shoes, made a bigger impact, then I'd finally feel worthy.
We gave away 100 million pairs of shoes. And I still felt empty.
Because I was tying my worth to what I achieved. And no amount of success can fill that void.
Feeling enough starts on the inside, not on a résumé.

#2: You can have everything and still feel broken.

When I sold TOMS, I had the money, the freedom everyone dreams about.
Six months later, I couldn't get out of bed. I was planning to end my life.
Success isn't the same thing as being okay.
Your mental health deserves the same attention as your goals.

#3: Purpose can't become your armor.

TOMS gave my life meaning. But I also used it to run from the pain I didn't want to face.
I told myself: "If I just help more people, maybe I'll finally feel enough."
But purpose can't heal a wound you refuse to look at.
When I sold the company and that purpose was gone? Everything I'd been running from caught up.
You can change the world and still feel broken inside.

#4: Your body will tell you when something's wrong.

The anxiety. The exhaustion. The voice in my head getting louder.
My body was screaming at me for years. I just kept ignoring it.
I told myself: "I'm fine. I'm busy. I'm building something important."
Until 2019, when the whispers turned into shouts.
Pay attention to the whispers before they turn into shouts.
You don't need to wait for a breakdown to make a change.

#5: Asking for help isn't weakness. It's wisdom.
I waited until I was at the edge before I reached out.
I thought I could handle it on my own. That asking for help meant I was failing.

But here's the truth: If negative thoughts last more than three days, call someone.

I'm 49 now, and I'm building “ENOUGH”, the most important thing I've ever created.

Not because I have to. But because it's what I needed when I was lost.
These lessons cost me 20 years of struggle. I'm sharing them so they don't cost you the same.

You don't need to achieve one more thing to be worthy.
You already are enough.

Do you feel like you're bad at making decisions? This might be why (it def was for me)...

In your work, you'll have to make two types of decisions: Complicated and Complex. 

Knowing which one you're making matters a lot...

Complicated decisions: Involve a lot of steps and require expertise, but there's always a "right" answer because there's a step by step process. Think things like writing code, filing taxes, running ROI calculations, etc.

Complex decisions: Have no definitive right or wrong answer. There's no process to follow. There are pros and cons to both sides. Think things like hiring/firing, choosing a strategy, creative design, etc.

Here's where people get stuck:

🧠 Our logical brains are really good at making complicated decisions.
😵‍💫 Our logical brains are really bad at making complex decisions.

When there is no clear "right" answer, our logical brains never land anywhere firm, and we get stuck in endless contemplation... 

Every pro has a con. Every step toward one side of the decision triggers a fear about not going with the other side. Back and forth we go. WEEEEE! The stress builds and builds. 

Another part of us shows up and says, JUST MAKE A DECISION ALREADY YA JERK! But you can't! The fear of making the wrong decision is too strong!

What's funny is that this doesn't even have to be a high-stakes decision like "what career path should I take?" I would sometimes get totally overwhelmed trying to decide which restaurant to go to! 

So how do we make complex decisions?

We have to get out of our heads and listen to our hearts, body, and guts. Here's a really simple exercise I often run with my clients:

Step 1: Close your eyes and think about going with Option A. 
Step 2: As you bring it into your awareness, track what happens in your body. Check with your heart. Check with your belly. Check with your whole body. Check with the field outside your body. See what you notice. Contraction? Relaxation? Energy? Excitement? Tension? Warmth? 
Step 3: Just notice and welcome the sensations. Breathe into them. Let them be there for a few minutes. 
Step 4: Then shake your body out to reset and do the same steps with Option B.

Sometimes just this simple exercise reveals the "right next step" for you to take. Your body might already know the answer. When you think of Option A, your you feel clear, expansive, loving, energized. When you think of Option B, you feel constricted, stuck, stressed, filled with fear.

Even if the answer doesn't become immediately clear, you'll have a different understanding of your experience with this decision. 

You'll feel and experience the fear more fully and realize it's okay. You can be with the fear on either side. Which is really what helps people make complex decisions with confidence. 

It's not that they know the answer. It's that they're surrendered to whatever happens, and trust that they can handle it.

There are more practices like this I do with clients, but start there. Hope it helps.

Patrick Phat Nguyen posted: Làm Product, viết spec sao cho đủ technical? Dạo gần đây, mình nhận được khá nhiều câu hỏi: “Không technical thì viết spec như thế nào để Dev hiểu?”, hoặc “Spec đã viết kỹ lắm rồi mà Design vẫn thiếu screen, QA vẫn thiếu case, còn Tech thì bảo chưa đủ technical?” Theo mình, vấn đề không hẳn nằm ở việc PM không biết code. Phần lớn vấn đề do PM chỉ mới mô tả ở tầng UI (màn hình có gì, user bấm vào đâu) nhưng chưa mô tả được hệ thống ở tầng Concept (thực thể gì, thuộc tính nào, và vận hành ra sao) Vấn đề này có 3 triệu chứng sau: (1) Bắt đầu bằng màn hình thay vì đối tượng nghiệp vụ: có flow và UI, nhưng chưa rõ dữ liệu, trạng thái và logic phía sau. (2) Mô tả ở tầng trừu tượng quá thấp (Quá technical) khiến team không hiểu feature sẽ generalize như thế nào, hoặc ở tầng trừu tượng quá cao (Quá high-level), không biết feature sẽ hành xử cụ thể ra sao. (3) Không thể mô tả và tư duy edge case của tính năng mà chỉ mô tả được happy case.
