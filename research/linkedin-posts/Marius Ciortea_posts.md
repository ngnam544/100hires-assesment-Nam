The most important community metric might be the least glamorous one.

Unanswered questions.

If too many posts sit there with no real answer, the rest of the dashboard starts to feel cosmetic.

That’s also how I’d judge a lot of "AI for community" rollouts right now.

Not by how polished the summaries look.
Not by how many AI features got turned on.
By whether the unanswered backlog actually goes down.

Because unanswered questions usually aren’t a moderation problem.
They’re an ops problem.

Usually some mix of:
- weak routing
- no clear owner
- expertise that exists but is hard to reach
- no escalation path for harder questions
- no loop that turns resolved answers into better docs

That’s where I think a lot of teams get stuck.
They treat unanswered questions like an annoying byproduct of scale.
They’re not.
They’re a trust tax.

Every unanswered thread teaches customers the same lesson:
this is not where I go to get help.

AI can help here.
I’m not skeptical of that.

It can triage.
It can suggest likely responders.
It can draft replies.
It can surface similar solved threads.

But it still won’t fix a community with no ownership, no response expectations, and no path for hard questions.

That’s why I think the better framing is simple:
AI is a multiplier.
It is not the foundation.

And if I were reporting community performance to leadership, I’d start with a handful of operating metrics:
- unanswered rate
- time to first response
- time to accepted answer
- repeat ask rate

Then I’d layer in careful deflection math.
Not fake precision. Just clear assumptions.

Some vendors are now selling directly against the unanswered gap.
Khoros, for example, has positioned Aurora AI around helping answer questions that previously went unanswered.
I’d treat numbers like that as vendor positioning, not proof.
But the message is still useful: buyers care about coverage.

Same with deflection benchmarks.
Higher Logic has shared rough starting assumptions like 1 in 5 views on accepted-answer threads and 1 in 20 for general pageviews.
Useful starting point, maybe.
Universal truth, definitely not.

My bias is pretty straightforward:
community teams should spend less time defending engagement metrics and more time proving question coverage.

Once you can show that the right questions got answered faster, the ROI conversation gets much easier.

What’s had the biggest impact for you: better routing, expert escalation, or a stronger docs loop?

Community should not sit off to the side of the customer experience.

This is 𝐏𝐚𝐫𝐭 1 of a 4-part interview series with Scott K. Wilder from LastPass on what it looks like when community becomes customer-experience infrastructure, not just a forum.

Part 1 starts with the operating model: what gets seeded, where time gets spent, and how community connects to the rest of the business.

𝐐𝐮𝐞𝐬𝐭𝐢𝐨𝐧:
How are you running the community day to day — what do you seed, where do you spend your time, and how does the community fit into the rest of what your team does?

Scott K. Wilder:
Privacy and security have become consequential for individuals, families, and businesses — a shift accelerated by AI-driven threats and a widening attack surface. LastPass is well positioned to be the authoritative place people go for credible answers in one location.

Day to day, my time splits across three areas: content curation and seeding (surfacing high-value topics from support signals before members need them), member engagement (making sure active threads get answered and strong contributors get recognized), and architecture (keeping LastPass Central organized so content from support, University, and sales materials is easy to find). We seed a blend of first-party and third-party content so the community stays relevant beyond the product itself.

Underneath it all is our voice-of-customer initiative, the Content Analyzer Platform (CAP), which mines support cases, chat transcripts, community questions, G2 reviews, and Reddit at once to learn the exact language customers use. We might write “one to many”; customers say “shared.” The goal is to meet them in their words, not ours — which makes the community more findable and more trusted.

What stood out to me: the strongest community teams are not just answering questions. They are learning how customers actually talk about their problems.

I keep coming back to that language gap: the words companies use internally are often not the words customers use when they need help.

Where does your community spend more time today: answering questions, or learning the customer’s language?

Stop asking what AI can do.

Ask what you'll let it do without you.

The moment a community becomes agent-connected, you're not just adding an AI feature.

You're delegating actions.

That's a different design problem.

I think the real product here is the permission ladder:

→ Always allow
→ Needs approval
→ Locked

Most teams will get this wrong by thinking in verbs.

Read feels safe. Write feels risky. Delete feels dangerous.

But risk isn't that neat.

Some reads are harmless.
Some reads expose private groups, mod notes, or member data.

Some writes are reversible.
Some create trust damage the second they go live.

So the ladder can't just be read vs write vs delete.

It has to be risk-based.

My default for community tools would look something like this:

Always allow:
• Public content lookup
• Summarizing public threads
• Tagging likely duplicates for review

Needs approval:
• Posting replies as the brand
• Editing member-facing content
• Changing workflows, access, or routing

Locked:
• Deletes
• Bans
• Bulk changes
• Anything that touches sensitive member data

This is why I don't think the moat will be the fanciest bot.

It'll be clear approvals, clean logs, and obvious ownership when something goes wrong.

Community is a messy UGC surface.

If agents can read posts, links, and messages, you should assume adversarial content shows up sooner than you want.

Spam, prompt injection, fake context, malicious links.

It's all part of the job now.

Operators are being pushed to become risk designers.

Not just feature buyers.

And honestly, that's probably the more durable skill.

If you run a community, support, or CX team, I'd start with one exercise:

Make your first draft of the permission ladder before you ship the agent.

What goes on your always allow list?
What needs approval?
What would you never let run unattended?

### Post 5
---
If your AI deflection number doesn’t come with CSAT and a policy-hallucination rate, it’s not deflection.

It’s debt.

A lot of teams are posting big automation wins right now.

50% deflection. Faster resolution. Leaner support.

What usually gets left out is the trust side of the ledger.

Because deflection is easy to juice if you let the bot answer too confidently, stretch beyond documented policy, or escalate too late.

The better metric is CSAT-protected deflection.

That means you only count the win if three things are true:
- CSAT stays inside an acceptable band
- policy-hallucination rate stays under a hard ceiling
- escalation rules are explicit enough that edge cases reach a human before they turn into repeat contacts, refunds, or compliance problems

That’s the part a lot of leaders miss.

Model choice matters, but it’s not the expensive part.

The expensive part is the operating discipline behind the rollout:
- auditing the knowledge base before launch
- separating repeatable intents from risky ones
- setting different confidence thresholds by category
- treating escalation like a product surface, not a fallback bucket

One composite operator case study I read recently made the point well: about 10k monthly tickets, around 60% concentrated in repeat archetypes, 51% deflection by month five, CSAT held within roughly +/-1.5 points, and policy hallucinations kept below 0.4%.

The important part wasn’t the model.

It was the sequence: knowledge audit, category-aware retrieval, gated pilot, then a clear escalation rubric.

That’s the playbook more teams should be publishing.

So if you’re leading AI support this quarter, I’d ask for a simple trust ledger:

CSAT band.
Policy-hallucination ceiling.
Escalation rubric.

If nobody can show those three things, the deflection number is telling you less than you think.

Customer Success and Community should be a lot closer than they usually are.
In most companies, CS owns the customer relationship and Community owns the customer conversation. But customers don't experience it that way. Nobody hits a wall and thinks, "ah, this is a CS problem" or "this one belongs in the community." They just have a problem.

CS sees what's happening inside an account. Community sees what's happening across all of them. One team notices an account has gone dark. When those signals stay in separate buckets, things slip through. You answer the same question privately over and over instead of turning it into something everyone can find. One of the best community MCP use cases in community is not a dashboard.

It is an early-warning list.

Start with a simple question: “Show me members who posted five or more times last quarter and have posted zero times this quarter.”

That alone is useful. But the better move is to layer signals.

Ask for their last login. Ask whether anyone replied to their last post. Ask whether they attended an event recently. Ask whether they opened support tickets, mentioned a product area, or belong to accounts your team cares about.

You can get this a few ways. Community activity only gives you a re-engagement list. Community plus CRM gives you account context. Community plus product usage gives you a much stronger read on whether someone went quiet because they solved their problem, lost interest, or got stuck.

The output should not just be a spreadsheet.

It should be a short list of people, why they matter, what changed, and the next best action.

That is where MCP becomes interesting for community teams. Not because it finds “inactive users,” but because it helps you ask a better question before those relationships disappear.

If I were using your community MCP to build a customer advisory board, I would not start with the loudest members.

I would start with overlap.

Who is asking thoughtful feature questions? Who is replying to other members? Who has accepted answers? Who shows product depth? Who represents an account or segment the business needs to understand better?

Any one of those signals can be misleading on its own.

The person who posts the most may not be the person with the best judgment. The person with the strongest product opinion may not be the one who helps others. The person who looks quiet in the community may be deeply active in events, support, or product feedback.

That is where MCP becomes useful.

Community data can identify helpers. CRM data can add account context. Product or support data can show where someone has real experience with the problem you are trying to solve.

The practical output is not “top members.”

It is a shortlist with a reason: invite this person because they help others, understand the product, represent this segment, and have shown sustained interest in this topic.

That is a much better starting point for community-led customer insight.

Another very practical MCP use case for community: unanswered questions.

Not “what is our engagement rate?”

A better question is: “Find questions from the last 30 days with zero or one reply, then group them by theme.”

That gives you a much more useful starting point.

From there, you can go deeper. Which themes show up most often? Which ones come from new members? Which ones come from high-value customers? Which questions are really support issues? Which ones are actually documentation gaps? Which ones point to product confusion?

You can answer this from community data alone, but it gets more useful when MCP can connect the dots across support tickets, documentation, search terms, product areas, and customer segments.

The output should not be “we had 42 unanswered questions.”

The output should be a reply queue, a content plan, and a product signal.

This is the shift I care about. MCP should not just help community teams report what happened. It should help them turn recurring questions into action.

If your community is about to get an AI agent, I’d do one boring thing before connecting anything:

draw the permission ladder.

Not the architecture diagram.
Not the demo flow.
The permission ladder.

Because the question is not just “can the agent connect?”

It’s:

What can it do when nobody is watching?

That’s where things get real.

My rough version would look like this:

Always allow:
- tightly scoped read actions
- summarizing public or approved spaces
- finding likely duplicates or related threads

Needs approval:
- posting replies
- tagging or pinning content
- changing workflows or routing
- anything member-facing

Locked:
- deletes
- bans
- permission changes
- bulk actions
- anything involving sensitive member data

The trap is thinking “read-only” automatically means safe.

It doesn’t.

A read-only agent can still search private spaces.
It can still summarize something sensitive.
It can still pull the wrong internal context into a moment where it does not belong.

So yes, reads should usually be easier than writes.
But they still need boundaries.

This is what I like about Circle’s MCP guidance. The split is practical: read-only tools can be set to always allow, while write and delete actions stay approval-gated.

OpenAI seems to be moving in the same direction with workspace agents: tool access, approvals, and monitoring are becoming part of the product conversation.

That feels right to me.

Not agents everywhere.
Not “let the AI handle it.”

Bounded autonomy.

Community teams already live with trust debt. Bad permissions just add interest.

So before piloting an agent in community or support ops, I’d want the first draft of the ladder in writing:

What is always allowed?
What needs approval?
What never runs unattended?

If you were rolling this out tomorrow, what would you lock down first: posting, member actions, or permissions?

If your AI rollout doesn't have a permissions tab, you didn't roll out AI.

You rolled out risk.

That's the part of the market I wish more people would say out loud.

We're still getting flooded with agent demos built to impress the room. Fast answers. Clean UI. A nice little moment where everyone nods and says, "wow."

But the real buying question usually isn't "how smart is it?"

It's "what exactly is this thing allowed to touch?"

That's why I think one of the most important AI features in 2026 is a boring admin screen.

Atlassian's Rovo MCP permissions setup is a good example of where the conversation actually needs to go. Admins can control what the server can read, write, and search. They can override rules per app. They can set defaults that carry forward to future additions. And those settings take precedence.

That's not a side detail. That's the operating model.

Because agents are not just a feature layer. They're an integration surface.

The minute an agent can read across systems, write into tools, or search internal context, you're in governance territory whether you like it or not.

And for support, CX, and community teams, the risk gets real fast.

A read only agent still might surface private escalations.
It still might pull internal notes into the wrong context.
It still might expose customer or member information that should have stayed buried behind a permission boundary.

So my default is pretty simple:

- read should be scoped
- write should be locked down hard
- anything that changes state should require approval
- the fallback path should be obvious when the agent can't answer or can't access something

If a vendor can't show that clearly, I don't care how polished the demo is.

I'd ask one question in the next AI vendor meeting:

Can you show me the exact read, write, and search defaults, plus what happens when the agent hits a permissions wall?

If the answer gets fuzzy, it's probably not ready for a real support or community environment.

What permission default do you trust by default: read, write, or neither?

My read is that this may be less about whether community “works” and more about whether the business goal changed.

Communities are usually strongest when the job is problem-solving, peer support, learning, belonging, and helping customers help each other.

The community may not have failed. It may simply have become the wrong tool for the new marketing job.

AI can save your community team time.

Cool.

But can it carry trust?

Because in community, automation isn't just "ops".
It's a relationship decision.

Who gets seen.
Who gets help.
Who gets warned.
Who gets ignored.

When teams roll out AI for moderation, routing, and engagement, the failure mode usually isn't throughput.

It's voice.
Bias.
Escalation.
Legitimacy.

A few operator rules I'd use if I was designing this today:

1) Treat escalation as a product, not a fallback.
If AI handles the frontline, the appeals path has to be obvious, fast, and owned.
Otherwise you're building relationship debt.

2) Kill the black box.
If members can't understand why something happened, they assume unfairness.
Transparency beats accuracy in the moment.

3) Assume bias shows up first in underrepresented voices.
They're easiest to misread.
They're easiest to suppress.
And communities feel that instantly.

4) Don't automate the community voice by default.
Let AI draft, summarize, and route.
But humans should own tone, exceptions, and relationship repair.

The cheapest sentence in support is "I don't know."

The most expensive one is a confident policy you never wrote.

That's the part a lot of teams still underestimate about AI support.

A wrong answer is annoying.
An invented rule is expensive.

The moment a bot says "this is our policy" about billing, access, bans, or account security, it stops being a search problem and becomes a trust problem.

That's why I think support teams need a harder line here:

If the bot can't cite the policy, it isn't allowed to decide the outcome.

It can help gather context.
It can summarize the issue.
It can suggest the next step.

But if the question affects someone's money, account access, or enforcement status, the safe move is simple:
refuse to guess, then escalate fast.

To me, that's the real design split:

- Low-stakes questions: answer directly, with citations
- High-stakes outcomes: assist, but route to a human with the evidence attached

And the handoff matters just as much as the refusal.

"Please contact support" is not an escalation system.

A real escalation flow should carry the conversation, the relevant account context, and the source material that shaped the answer so the human can pick it up without making the customer start over.

Most teams are still measuring the wrong thing.

Deflection gets tracked.
Resolution time gets tracked.
Ticket volume gets tracked.

Those numbers matter.

But I'd want one more metric on the dashboard:

How often was the bot allowed to make a policy-shaped claim without a source?

That's the number I'd worry about.

Because once the bot speaks in the company voice, people don't hear "experimental system."
They hear "official answer."

And when that answer turns out to be made up, the cleanup cost is way higher than the few minutes you saved by avoiding a handoff.

If you run support, CX, or product:
what's one category your bot is not allowed to answer without escalation?
