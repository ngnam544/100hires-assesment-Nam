### Post 3
Herzliya Pituach is one of the most underrated places in Israel for people working in tech.

Here’s why:

Most people associate Israeli tech with Tel Aviv.

What many people don't realize is that before Sarona became the center of the startup ecosystem, a huge amount of Israel's tech industry was based in Herzliya.

And a lot of that ecosystem is still there today.

Apple, Microsoft, AppsFlyer, and a number of prominent venture capital firms like Pitango, Viola Ventures, and Glilot Capital all have a significant presence in the area.

The second advantage is accessibility.

Herzliya Pituach is an incredible location if you work in Tel Aviv.

It's roughly a 15-18 minute drive without traffic and even during rush hour, it's often closer to 25-30 minutes than an hour.

And in fact, the Tel Aviv light rail is being extended to Herzliya, which will make the connection even easier.

The third advantage is that people are willing to come to you because Herzliya Pituach is such a beautiful place. As I covered in my previous post, between the beach, restaurants, and atmosphere, it's an appealing place to spend some time.

I had numerous meetings in Herzliya where people travelled in from Tel Aviv, Ra'anana, and elsewhere in central Israel.

So if you work in tech, moving to Herzliya Pituach might be a great option for you.

It's home to many of the companies and investors that have built the tech ecosystem and it's still one of the easiest places from which to access Tel Aviv.

### Post 4
Herzliya Pituach was the final community we tried out.

And it highlighted something really important. When choosing where to live, you're often balancing two different things:

Lifestyle and community.

When it comes to lifestyle, Herzliya Pituach is hard to beat.

You have the amazing beaches, great restaurants, easy access to Tel Aviv, and a more relaxed atmosphere. There’s still plenty of energy around you, but without the high intensity that comes with living in central Tel Aviv.

I can absolutely see why so many people love it here.

However, from my experience, this may not be as strong a community for 30-55 year old Anglos moving to Israel without a strong existing network.

First, it’s a lot more ‘Israeli’ than all the other communities I tried out.

There are diplomats, Israelis, Anglos, and people from all over the world. It’s less of an Anglo bubble and more like a diverse international community. For many this is ideal.

Second, my impression was that most people here already have their established circles, friendships, and routines.

No one was trying to ‘recruit’ us like countless people tried to in places like Zichron Yaakov and Tel Mond.

That’s not necessarily a criticism, and for many it is a positive. It's just a different dynamic here.

And after spending time in so many different communities, I understand even more clearly that there’s no such thing as a perfect community.

Only the community that’s the best fit for you.

In my next post, I'll share why Herzliya Pituach might actually be one of the most underrated places in Israel for people working in tech.

### Post 7
We spent 6 weeks living in Zichron Yaakov.

What’s crazy is that it wasn’t even on our original list.

So here’s the backstory. While in Jerusalem, I met Eric Zimmerman, and he asked me what I was looking for in a community.

I told him, a bucolic setting, a nice Anglo community, etc and he said “Have you thought about Zichron Yaakov?”

I told him no. I had visited years ago and didn’t feel like it was my vibe.

He said, “David, you’re making a huge mistake. You’ve got to give it a chance.”

So credit to Eric, he was 100% right.

If I had to describe it simply, it feels like a great mix between Caesarea and Ra’anana.

It has that out-of-town, beautiful setting like Caesarea, but with a much more active and social community similar to Ra’anana.

Let’s get into what I really liked about it:

1. Everyone loves Zichron
Whenever I met someone and told them we were planning to try out Zichron Yaakov, the reaction was always the same: “Oh Zichron? I love Zichron!”

It seems to have a universally positive reputation across Israel. It gave us a very good feeling going in.

2. The beauty and outdoors
It is probably the most picturesque Anglo city within an hour commute of Tel Aviv.

And if you’re into the outdoors, it’s very hard to beat. Between the Carmel forest, Ramat HaNadiv, the proximity to the beach, and just the sheer number of trails, you could easily spend a decade hiking within an hour of Zichron and not run out of options.

3. The history
Zichron is one of the early centers of modern Israel and is home to the First Aliyah Museum. It’s a place that played a real role in the founding of the country.

Having just moved to the country, it was quite special to live in a place where some of the first waves of immigrants came to build a life.

4. The people
Living in Zichron is like living in the TV show Cheers; it’s a place where everyone knows your name. You feel part of the community very quickly.

In larger cities like Tel Aviv or Ra’anana, you can stay invisible for a long time. In Zichron, after a few weeks it felt like every time I walked in the city center, I bumped into someone I knew.

5. The Friday nights
Every Friday night, in the main synagogue in Zichron, there’s a communal gathering where people from different religious backgrounds and countries of origin come together.

You have people dressed in black and white sitting next to people in t-shirts and jeans. It’s quite special to be part of.

I’m just glad I listened to my friend Eric, as it was such an incredible experience.

If you live or have ever lived in Zichron, have I done it justice?

### Post 8
Why is shopping more important than education?

This was my biggest frustration with the Israeli government during this war. And I am sharing now in the likely event war returns…

It looks like no one has learned anything from COVID.

We have so much evidence about the negative impact on children, teenagers, and university students when they’re not in school, and yet Israel went straight back to the same approach of fully remote learning in most schools near Tel Aviv.

At the same time as schools have been closed, you can walk through malls and see thousands of people, or go to gyms with hundreds of people working out. Life, in many ways, is continuing.

But when it comes to education, everything automatically moves to Zoom.

I understand the concern around safety. No one wants a mass casualty event. But we don’t want that in a mall either, so it’s hard to understand why one is allowed and the other isn’t.

If a school has classes of 15–20 students and access to a shelter, there should have been ways to make it work.

You can rotate students, split classes, alternate weeks, bring in half the grades at a time, and use space more creatively.

It won’t be perfect, but it’s far better than fully remote learning.

As someone teaching at Tel Aviv University, I can say clearly that Zoom is a worse experience for students. It’s less engaging, less interactive, and it has a real impact over time.

We need to create a system with protocols that allows education to continue in a structured, safe way.

People need to work, and children need to be in school. It’s not realistic to expect families to sit at home indefinitely every time something happens.

This is too important to keep getting wrong.

Who is actually taking responsibility to fix this?

### Post 9
After reflecting on the 40 day war, I’m hoping it is done but realistically there will be another in months or years ahead. So, hopefully these will not be needed but wanted to share some personal tips:

1. It’s okay to take a break if you can

Many Israelis are being called back to reserves, have young kids at home, or have jobs that cannot be done remotely. For many, it is impossible to leave the country for a few days.

But if you have the ability, it’s okay to take a short break, leave the country, recharge - and realize how much despite the chaos and sirens, you’d still rather be in Israel than anywhere else.

I left for 6 days. I needed a mental break. But returned even more proud to be a new Israeli.

2. Live life to the max

There are 2 different philosophies people live by. Some people have hunkered down and barely left their house since it started. Others try to live life to its fullest.

I’m in the second group.

I’m not willing to sacrifice a minute of living life to the fullest, but I do it in a safe environment.

For example, I love to jog. I used to run by the beach. Now I run in the city so I’m always close to a shelter.

You’ll need to adjust, but just keep living.

3. Don’t read the news

I’ve stopped reading political news completely.

I don’t believe most of it, so why bother reading something I don’t believe?

Either it gets your hopes up that it’s ending soon and that doesn’t happen so you get despondent, or it makes you feel like this is never ending and the situation is hopeless.

Either way, it’s a lose-lose.

I’ve found it much better to just focus on other things that I can actually control.

Those are my top 3.

If you have others, please share in the comments!

### Post 12
New city alert: We just spent a month living in Tel Mond - a 14,000 person town roughly between Ra’anana and Netanya.

When I shared my list of locations with friends the common response was, all those places are typical, but Tel Mond wasn’t. Incredibly glad we were there.

A bit of background on Tel Mond. The vast majority of the community are actually home-grown Israelis. The Anglo community was founded 25 years ago by a dozen South African families.

They were living in raanana and wanted to find a nearby location where they could get more bang (aka square footage) for their buck.

Now for my experience in Tel Mond:

1. People notice you!
Tel Mond is the smallest city we’ve stayed in so far. It’s like a sixth the size of Ra’anana. You can walk from one end to the other in about half an hour. The positive is that within the first few days, a dozen people came over and asked if I was new. In some of the other places we stayed, it took weeks before people realized we were new to the area.

2. It’s incredibly bucolic
Because it’s smaller, there’s beautiful greenery everywhere. The town is surrounded by orchards. Orange trees, strawberry fields, fruit growing in almost every yard. I’d go for runs and literally pick oranges off trees along the way.

3. The Anglo community is incredibly tight-knit
There’s essentially one main Anglo synagogue with over 200 families. People stay after services for often two plus hours each week. Kids run around together. Parents talk. And if you’re gone for a couple of weeks, people will ask about you.

4. It’s very quiet at night
I have to be honest, for someone that likes to go out in the evenings there isn’t much to do here at night. It’s not a “walk out your door at 9:30pm and see what’s happening” kind of place. Ra’anana is about a 12-minute drive away. Netanya isn’t far either. So you’re not isolated, but you’re driving most of the time if you want a night out.

What started out as a bit of a left-field choice on our list of communities to try out turned into a really meaningful experience.

And I’ll leave with this huge statement:

I have not seen a tighter-knit Anglo community in Israel than the Mevaseret Zion community of Tel Mond.

Tel Monders - did I do your community justice?

Is “friend profiling” okay?

We all do it. We meet people and all-too-quickly decide based on look, speech, age, dress, song, dance, whatever, whether we want to engage or abandon the conversation.

Often that first impression is proven mistaken (and I love when that happens), yet we continue to “friend profile.”

After moving to Israel and visiting 7 cities for 4-6 weeks at a time to find our community I have found a consistent hack that helps in getting me excited to quickly get to know others in a more meaningful way.

I’ll share our adventurous year plans and if the response is, “wow that sounds tiring,” we likely aren’t meant to be besties. If the response is, “wow, I’ve always wanted to do that,” then consistently I’ve had a real connection with that person.

### Post 18
“You can’t work in tech and live in Caesarea. It’s too far north.”

This is simply not true.

The key is the Binyamina train station.

The high-speed train gets you to Tel Aviv University in 28 minutes, and Caesarea is only about a 13–15 minute drive from the station.

Once you factor that in, the commute looks very different.

In some cases, commuting from the “closer” cities can actually take longer.

From Ra’anana, for example, it can take over 30 minutes by car just to reach the Herzliya train station.

Which is why Caesarea, along with nearby neighbourhoods like Binyamina and Pardes Chana, has quietly attracted a strong tech crowd.

Yes there aren’t many offices or companies in Caesarea itself, but there are plenty of founders, operators, and investors who choose to live there.

I went to a barbecue in Pardes Chana hosted by @Yoel Israel, and over half the people I met worked in tech or VC.

From a networking standpoint, that’s meaningful.

You may not be surrounded by office towers, but you’re surrounded by people who are deeply connected to the ecosystem and intentionally choose these communities for the balance they offer.

The commute is easier and much shorter than people assume, and the local network is much stronger than you’d expect.

And as more tech talent moves into the area, I wouldn’t be surprised if companies eventually open up there.

So can you work in tech and live in Caesarea (or nearby Pardes Chana, Binyamina, Or Akiva or Hadera)?

Absolutely.

### Post 21
After 5+ weeks in Caesarea, many have asked me “Should I move to Caesarea?”

The hot take:

Caesarea is not the right fit if you…

1. Prefer living in a place where “everyone looks like you.”
If you want the familiar Anglo clusters that include families with young children that you find in Ra’anana, Modiin, or Jerusalem, you won’t find that here. Caesarea typically attracts an Anglo demographic whose children have already moved out (but love to visit!) and far fewer Americans who originate from New York. (Active work is being done to help expand educational options for children however, so it’s certainly worth monitoring!)

2. Want nightlife or a real downtown
There isn’t one. Stores are spread out in small clusters, and most restaurants close early on Saturday night. If you enjoy going out at night, living here will be challenging for you.

3. Want a dense religious or educational ecosystem
There are daily services, but far fewer classes, lectures, and community events compared to larger Anglo areas. If you want a full calendar of religious programming, you won’t find that here.

4. Prefer a neighborhood that always feels full
A lot of people use Caesarea as a second home. Some streets can feel quite quiet during the week.

Caesarea is a great fit if you…

1. Want a community that genuinely wants you there.
People welcome newcomers quickly and warmly. There’s real openness to letting new families take on leadership, help shape the community, and get involved right away. And many of the members are incredibly philanthropic not just with financial support but with their time.

2. Value nature, space, and character.
Caesarea has mature trees, ancient sites, and a landscape that is different from the more built-up parts of Israel. If you like quiet beauty, this is one of the best places for it.

3. Like having lots of kosher food options.
Surprisingly, there are dozens of kosher restaurants within 15 minutes driving and a lot of them are excellent.

4. Want a calmer Anglo environment.

### Post 30
I left my last role as CEO in February 2024.

And it’s been a lot harder than I thought.

When you spend decades building your career, the rhythm becomes familiar.

You move from one milestone to the next. From manager to director, from VP to SVP, eventually to CEO.

That momentum becomes part of who you are. You’re always focused on what’s next.

So when the day comes and you step away from the CEO role, it is not just the end of a chapter, it is the end of a way of thinking and being.

And that can be far more difficult than people realize.

After I sold Meetup, many people asked me what I was going to do next. For a long time, I didn’t have an answer.

I had been so conditioned to chase the next opportunity that sitting still felt uncomfortable. In fact, it felt unnatural.

That discomfort, I’ve come to learn, is common.

Many former CEOs immediately look for the next big thing. Not because they are ready, but because they don’t know how to pause.

Fortunately, I received some valuable advice: take time. Six months. A year. Resist the urge to jump back in too quickly. Give yourself space to reflect and to rediscover what matters most.

Pausing is not easy when your instincts are built around forward motion. But that pause is essential.

It creates room to ask important questions.
- What do I care about now?
- Where do I want to spend my time?
- Who do I want to serve?

There is also a shift in identity.

Without the title, the invitations stop, your influence changes, and your ego has to adjust.

The sooner you can accept that - and embrace new ways of connecting and contributing - the sooner you can find meaning again.

In my case, stepping back created new opportunities for growth.

It forced me to let go of the mindset that success means always chasing the next milestone.

It reminded me that sometimes, the most important thing you can do is pause and simply be.

Learning that - and reshaping how I see success and identity - has been one of the hardest, and most rewarding, lessons of this new phase in my life.
